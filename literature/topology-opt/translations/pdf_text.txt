Minimum length scale control in structural topology optimization
based on the Moving Morphable Components (MMC) approach

Weisheng Zhang, Dong Li, Jian Zhang and Xu Guo1

State Key Laboratory of Structural Analysis for Industrial Equipment,
Department of Engineering Mechanics,
Dalian University of Technology, Dalian, 116023, P.R. China

Abstract
A novel approach, which can control the minimum length scale in topology optimization in a straightforward
and explicit way, is proposed in the present paper. This approach is constructed under the so-called Moving
Morphable Components (MMC) based solution framework where optimized structural topology can be found by
changing the shapes and layout of a set of trapezoid-shaped structural components on a fixed finite element mesh.
The eXtended Finite Element Method (XFEM) is employed for structural response analysis and shape sensitivity
information is obtained by carrying out numerical integration along the structural boundary. A precision definition
of the minimum length scale of a structure is also suggested based on the proposed solution framework. Compared
with existing approaches, the proposed approach can achieve minimum length scale control by simply setting lower
bounds on a set of geometric design variables. Only a small number of purely geometric constraints imposed on the
sizes of intersection regions should be considered explicitly in order to get a complete minimum length scale
control. Numerical examples demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

Keywords: Topology optimization; Moving Morphable Components (MMC); Minimum length scale; Shape
optimization.

Structural topology optimization, a computational method aiming at designing innovative structures by
distributing a given amount of material within a design domain appropriately, has developed into a relatively
mature stage since the seminal contributions of Rozvany [1], Cheng and Olhoff [2], Bendsøe and Kikuchi [3].
Nowadays, in addition to mechanical problems, topology optimization has also found its applications in various
physical disciplines including acoustics, electromagnetics and optics, just to name a few. We refer the readers to
[4–7] and the references therein for a state-of-the-art review of the recent developments in topology optimization.
Although remarkable achievements have been made in topology optimization, there still exist some
challenging problems which have not been well addressed. One of them is how to control the minimum length scale
of a structure (i.e., minimum feature size of a structure) in an explicit and local way [6]. Generally speaking, the
minimum structural length scale is corresponding to the minimum cross-sectional diameter of a rod, the minimum
thickness of a plate and the minimum radius of a set of circular holes in a continuum structure, just to name a few.
Although controlling the minimum length scale was initially proposed to regularize the naive formulation for
topology optimization of continuum structures and eliminate the numerical instabilities such as checkerboard
phenomenon, it has been increasingly realized that this issue should also be considered seriously when the
manufacturability/stability of a structural member or the robustness of a structure is taken into consideration [8–12].
This is because a structural component with too small cross sectional areas is prone to buckle even under small
magnitude of compressive force and the appearance of tiny-hinges in compliant mechanism designs may lead to
unexpected breaking of the structure during a manufacture process.
A lot of efforts have been devoted to control the minimum length scale in topology optimization. Within the
Solid Isotropic Material with Penalization (SIMP) framework, Sigmund [13] proposed a sensitivity filter scheme to
eliminate mesh-dependent phenomenon and checkerboard patterns in topology optimization. This scheme has also
been found to have the capability of controlling the structural minimum length scale in an implicit way through the
radius of filter. It is also observed that the slope-constrained formulation for topology optimization [14], where
point-wise bounds on density slopes are considered to ensure the existence of solutions, can also control the
minimum length scale implicitly. Paulsen [8] proposed a so-called MOLE (MOnotonicity based minimum LEngth
scale) method to carry out minimum length scale control in topology optimization. The key idea is to introduce a
global functional

Guest [15] suggested a minimum length scale control approach (Heaviside Projection Method (HPM)) by using
nodal density values as primary design variables. In this approach, a projection operator is introduced to project the
nodal values of density field onto the element space in order to determine the element density used for stiffness
interpolation. Later on, the HPM approach had also been extended in a series of works to account for more general
cases [16–18]. Minimum length scale control has also been studied under the level set-based topology optimization
framework. For example, by introducing a non-local energy functional describing the interactions between different
points on structural boundary into the objective functional, Chen [10] found that thin elongate structures with fixed
widths can be obtained under the corresponding problem formulation. The same approach was also used by Luo
[19] to design hinge-free compliant mechanisms in a subsequent study. Besides the above approaches, the perimeter
constraint approaches [20–23], wavelet-based methods [24, 25] and robust topology optimization formulations [26]
can also be used to control the minimum structural length scale in a global and implicit way. Detailed discussions
on the advantages and problems associated with the above approaches can be found in [27].
As pointed out in [27], all of the above mentioned approaches control the minimum structural length scale in
an implicit way. Recent years also witnessed numerous attempts made for solving the same problem explicitly. Guo
et al. proposed a level set-based approach which can achieve a complete, explicit and local length scale control in
topology optimization [27]. In this approach, the medial line (for two-dimensional case)/medial surface (for
three-dimensional case) of a structure and signed distance level set function are used simultaneously to give precise
definitions of its minimum and maximum length scales. Based on the proposed definitions, minimum/maximum
length scale control can be achieved by imposing the lower/upper bound on the minimum/maximum value of the
signed distance level set function on the set of the medial line/surface of the structure. Furthermore, a systematic
numerical solution approach was also established in this work. The same idea was also implemented in the
SIMP-based topology optimization framework for explicit length scale control [12] and applied successfully to
control the layout of structural systems with multiple embedding components [28]. Inspired by the idea in [27], Xia
and Shi proposed an explicit length scale control approach by using the trimmed structural skeleton (medial
line/surface) and constraining the distance from the boundary [29, 30] to the skeleton [31]. Later on, by identifying
the inflection regions of solid and void phases through a so-called structural indicator function, Zhou et al. put
forward a approach, which can obtain optimized topology designs with user-specified minimum length scale using
a three-field filtering-threshold topology optimization scheme [32]. Another systematic study on signed distance
level set function based length scale control method can be found in [33]. In this work, under the assumption that
the boundary of a structure is

Although numerous methods have already been developed to achieve explicit structural length scale control,
there is still room for further improvement. For example, in [27–32], extra computational cost must be paid to
extract the structural skeletons and the skeleton is only differentiable with respect to shape parameters when the
boundary of a structure is at least

smooth. In Allaire et al.’s approach [33], a double layer integration must be

calculated to obtain the value of minimum length scale constraint function. This treatment is obviously
computationally expensive from numerical implementation point of view. Furthermore, the value of the minimum
length scale constraint function is not well defined and in general cannot be evaluated numerically at a non-smooth
point of the boundary since it involves an internal optimization process to find the “worst-case” unit vector in the
subdifferential at that point. Another problem associated with these methods is that a large number of point-wise
constraints must be aggregated in order to make the numerical solution process computationally tractable. Under
this circumstance, the aggregation schemes must be constructed very carefully otherwise a precise point-wise
length scale control cannot be achieved. Last but not least, as demonstrated in many works, including the length
scale constraints in topology optimization (especially in a hard way, which means that the constraints are imposed
without any relaxation) may prevent topological changes in the process of optimization and therefore often leads to
trapping in local optima.
The objective of the present paper is to propose a new approach which can control the minimum length scale
in topology optimization through a straightforward way. In this approach, unlike in all previous treatments,
minimum length scale control is mainly achieved by simply setting lower bounds on a set of geometric design
variables. Only a small number of purely geometric constraints should be considered explicitly in order to get a
complete minimum length scale. Our approach is constructed based on a newly developed topology optimization
framework, i.e., the Moving Morphable Components (MMC) based framework, where a set of structural
components are used as the basic building blocks of optimization [34–37]. We shall give a detail description of this
approach in Section 3. For the sake of simplicity, only two-dimensional problem is considered in the present study.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we present the basic idea of doing topology
optimization in MMC-based solution framework. Then a novel minimum length scale control method is put
forward in Section 3. Numerical implementation issues are addressed in Section 4. In Section 5, some numerical
examples are presented to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. Finally some concluding remarks
are provided in the last section.

2. MMC-based topology optimization framework
Since the proposed approach closely relies on the so-called MMC-based topology optimization framework, a
brief introduction of the basic idea of MMC will be given first.
As described in [34], unlike in traditional approaches where structural topologies are represented by element
densities (in SIMP approach) or nodal values of a level set function (in level set approach), in MMC-based
approach, a set of moving morphable components are adopted as basic building blocks of topology optimization
(see Fig. 1 for an illustration). These components are allowed to move, deform, overlap and merge in the design
domain freely, and optimized structural topology can be obtained by changing the positions, inclined angles,
lengths widths and the layout of these components. This treatment provides a new paradigm for topology
optimization and has big potential to resolve some challenging issues that cannot be dealt with easily by traditional
methods. We refer the readers to [34–37] for more detailed discussions on the merits of this approach.
As discussed in [34–37], there are basically two kinds of ways to describe the geometry of a structural
component in MMC framework. One is achieved by constructing an explicit topology description function (TDF)
under Eulerian description framework (an elegant way to construct the TDF of a structural component with curved
shape skeleton/medial surface is proposed in [37]) and the other is achieved by using a set of parameters to
characterize the geometry of the components in a pure Lagrangian way. In the present work, the latter treatment is
adopted. Under this circumstance, the topology optimization problem can be formulated in the MMC framework

In the present approach, we need to determine the relative positions of different components (e.g., intersecting

Thanks to the advancement of modern computational geometry, these operations can be implemented robustly by
well-established algorithms. We refer the readers to [39] for more details on this aspect.

optimization framework, the XFEM approach proposed in [40] is adopted for structural and sensitivity analysis
(calculating the primary and adjoint displacement fields). The key point of this approach is to re-construct the finite
element (FE) mesh around the structural boundary based on the nodal values of the TDF function (i.e.,
through linear interpolation. We refer the readers to [35] for more numerical implementation details. It is worth
noting that since exact geometry information of the structural boundary is available in the proposed approach,
non-uniform body-fitted FE mesh (See Fig. 9 for reference) can also be constructed adaptively to obtain the values
of stress and strain along structural boundary with high degree of accuracy and reduce the degrees of freedom in FE

4.5 Treatment of the point-wise minimum length scale constraints
As observed in previous works [12, 27], the existence of the minimum length scale constraints may hinder the
formation of load carrying structures especially at initial stages of optimization. This is because in these approaches,
optimized structural topology is typically found through a degenerating process where unnecessary subregions are
eliminated from the design domain by shrinking their minimum length scales to zero values. This process, however,
will inevitably violate the minimum length scale constraints. Therefore, in these approaches, the minimum length
scale constraints are either imposed in a soft way [12, 27] or imposed only after an appropriate topology has been
formed.
In the present work, however, the minimum length scale constraints for each component (i.e.,
and

respectively) can be imposed in a hard way (i.e., without any relaxation). In contrast to

the previous approaches, this treatment will not prevent the components from forming a load carrying structure
since in MMC-based topology optimization framework topology change can also be achieved through a component
hiding mechanism (see Fig. 10 for reference). This means that the unnecessary components can make no
contribution to overall stiffness of the structure even though their characteristic length scales are not taking small
values! The minimum length scale constraints on the intersection regions of components, however, should be
imposed in a soft way (i.e.,
